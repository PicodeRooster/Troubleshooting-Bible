# Cable Management

Poor cable management is more than an eyesore. Tangled, unorganized cables obstruct airflow in systems and enclosures, leading to overheating. They create tripping hazards in office spaces and data centers. And when something goes wrong, a rat's nest of unlabeled wires makes troubleshooting and maintenance significantly harder. Good cable management prevents all of this — and most of what it takes costs very little.

---

## Fastening Cables in Place

### Coaxial Cable Staples or Cord Tubing

Coaxial cable staples — also called cable wire clips, nail-ins, or polyethylene coaxial staples — are small C-shaped plastic fasteners with a notch on one side for a nail. They hold cables flush against a wall, baseboard, or surface, and work well for running cables up and around doorways or window frames. A pack of 100 typically costs around $5.

An alternative is plastic cord tubing (often sold under the brand name Cordmate). Tubing encases cables entirely and includes joints for turns. It costs more and takes longer to install, but provides a cleaner finished look in visible areas.

### Adhesive Hooks

**Personal systems (life hack):** Command Brand hooks from 3M are a convenient, damage-free option for home and personal setups. They adhere to virtually any surface — the underside of a desk, the back of a media cabinet, a wall — and remove cleanly without leaving marks or holes. Use the cord clip style to guide cables along surfaces and keep them out of the way. The advantage over nail-in clips is that you can remove or swap cables without removing the hooks. Expect to pay around $6 for a pack of four.

**Enterprise environments:** For professional or commercial installations, use non-trace hanging hooks to fasten cables above the floor. These keep cables elevated, prevent them from sliding around underfoot, and eliminate tripping hazards without leaving permanent marks on walls or raised-floor tiles.

## Bundling and Organizing Active Cables

### Zip Ties and Velcro One-Wraps

Zip ties (also called cable ties) are the fastest way to bring a bundle of cables under control. They're ideal for grouping multiple cords running out of the same surge protector, along the back of a desk, or through a rack. When a cable is significantly longer than needed, loop the excess once or twice before securing it. Take care not to cinch zip ties too tightly, as over-tightening can stress the wiring inside.

Velcro One-Wraps are a reusable alternative — small strips of hook-and-loop fabric that bundle cables the same way zip ties do, but can be undone and repositioned without cutting. They're especially useful for smaller cables like phone chargers that you carry or swap out frequently. Labeled Velcro ties (as used in professional environments) serve double duty: they bundle and identify cables at the same time.

### Painter's Tape

When zip ties and Velcro aren't available, painter's tape or masking tape works as a short-term solution to hold cables against a desk, wall, or the underside of a surface. It doesn't hold indefinitely — longevity depends on the surface type and humidity — but it's safe on delicate finishes. For extra-sensitive surfaces, press a length of tape against your skin a few times before applying it to reduce its adhesive strength.

## Power Strip Placement

An insufficient number of power outlets is one of the root causes of cable sprawl. Power strips and surge protectors consolidate plug-ins and reduce the number of cables running across open floor space. Mount them to the underside of a desk or the back of a hutch using double-sided mounting tape or Velcro strips to keep them off the floor and out of sight.

## Labeling and Documentation

Unlabeled cables cause delays during troubleshooting and increase the risk of accidentally disconnecting the wrong device under pressure. Labeling is one of the highest-value habits in cable management.

**Label maker:** A basic label maker (~$30) covers most needs. To wrap a label cleanly around a cable, enter text followed by a long blank space, then fold the label so the sticky sides meet around the cord — one side shows the text, the other is blank. Alternatively, enter the text twice with a double space in between so it appears on both sides.

**DIY option:** Bread bag tags are a free alternative for cables that aren't in public view. They're not professional-looking, but they're effective for identifying cords on a crowded surge protector or behind a router.

**Color coding:** For environments with many cables of the same type, color-coded ties or sleeves allow at-a-glance identification without reading individual labels. In data centers and server rooms, color coding combined with a written or digital cable map makes tracing connections significantly faster during incidents.

## Storing Unused Cables

Unused cables left loose in a drawer or bin quickly tangle and become difficult to identify. Before storing, wrap each cable individually using one of the following:

- **Velcro One-Wraps or Velcro cable ties:** Wrap the cable into a loose coil and secure it with the tie. Reusable and easy to undo without cutting.
- **Zip ties:** Use a single loosely fastened zip tie around a coiled cable. Leave enough slack that the cable isn't kinked or stressed.
- **Over-under technique:** For longer cables like extension cords or HDMI cables, use the over-under coiling method before securing — this preserves the cable's natural twist and prevents internal wire fatigue over time.

Store wrapped cables in labeled bins, pouches, or drawers organized by cable type (e.g., power, display, USB, audio). This makes retrieval straightforward and keeps unused inventory from becoming its own tangle problem.

---

## Safety Reminders

- Never run cables under carpets or rugs — foot traffic wears through insulation over time and creates a fire hazard.
- Do not bend or wind cables repeatedly or too tightly; this damages the internal wiring.
- Keep cables and fastening materials away from outlets and heat sources.
- The practices in this guide apply to consumer-level cables and cords. Do not modify building electrical wiring unless you are a licensed electrician.
